# Why are soap bubbles round?

(adult-level science text — to be paraphrased for *Wonder Lab Kids* #15)

A soap bubble is a thin film of water sandwiched between two layers of
soap molecules. The film is held together by *surface tension* — the
tendency of liquid surfaces to minimise their area. Among all closed
shapes that contain the same volume of air, the sphere has the smallest
surface area, so the film naturally relaxes into a sphere as the surface
tension pulls every part of the film inward equally.

Three follow-on points:

- The soap molecules reduce the water's surface tension just enough that
  the film can stretch without breaking.
- A bubble that is not a sphere — say, the moment you blow it out of the
  wand — is unstable. It oscillates and quickly settles into a sphere.
- If two bubbles meet and merge, the wall between them flattens until the
  shared geometry minimises the total surface area. Plateau's laws.
